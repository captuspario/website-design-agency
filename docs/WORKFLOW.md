# Workflow

## Stage 1: Business context intake

- Gather business model, services, offers, locations, goals, and trust signals.
- Confirm scope constraints and decision owners.
- Approval: project-manager confirms intake is complete.

## Stage 2: Market and competitor analysis

- Review direct and adjacent competitors.
- Capture category patterns, weak clichés, trust cues, and differentiation opportunities.
- Approval: leadership confirms the research direction is usable.

## Stage 3: Audience and journey insight

- Infer audience groups, priorities, objections, and common tasks.
- Produce a lightweight journey model focused on decisions, not exhaustive personas.
- Approval: product-designer accepts the audience model as good enough to design with.

## Stage 4: Content and messaging direction

- Define message hierarchy, page intent, proof needs, and CTA logic.
- Mark confirmed facts versus inference.
- Approval: leadership or client-side reviewer signs off on messaging direction.

## Stage 5: Information architecture and page blueprint

- Recommend sitemap changes, navigation simplification, and page-type decisions.
- Define page blueprint expectations for key templates.
- Approval: creative-director and project-manager approve the IA package.

## Stage 6: UX and UI direction

- Turn approved strategy into page flow, section order, component choices, and visual rules.
- Keep the design direction realistic for the WordPress baseline.
- Approval: creative-director approves the design direction before build.

## Stage 7: Build readiness gate

- Confirm approved copy direction, image shortlist, and build constraints.
- Resolve major blockers before assembly starts.
- Approval: project-manager issues build-ready sign-off.

## Stage 8: WordPress assembly

- Build in local or staging WordPress only.
- Use approved theme, approved plugins, and reusable patterns first.
- Approval: wordpress-builder hands off to QA only when the build is stable enough to review.

## Stage 9: Multi-pass QA

- Run UX, accessibility, technical, SEO, analytics, and handover checks.
- Fix issues or return upstream if structural changes are required.
- Approval: quality team signs off when blocking issues are cleared.

## Stage 10: Final approval

- Confirm the package is coherent, usable, and within scope.
- Review unresolved risks and minor follow-up items.
- Approval: leadership gives final approval.

## Stage 11: Reporting and handover

- Produce compact handover and summary documents.
- Record what changed, what still needs attention, and what the owner should manage next.
- Approval: handover-reviewer confirms owner readiness.
